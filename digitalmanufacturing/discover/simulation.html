What is
Plant Simulation?

Plant Simulation is a discrete-event simulation software by Siemens (formerly Tecnomatix). It enables engineers to create digital models of manufacturing systems, simulate their behaviour over time, and analyse key performance indicators such as throughput, utilisation, and cycle times (Bangsow, 2020).

Printing Farm
Model Architecture

Adding the 3D printing farm introduced the core modelling challenge: a printer produces 3 casing sets simultaneously on a single build plate. However, Plant Simulation’s ParallelStation is designed around a one-part-at-a-time logic. Representing the process with a single station and a container of six parts caused inconsistencies, since the system interpreted each item as an individual component rather than as paired parts belonging to one cellphone. As a result, several simplifications were implemented to preserve the realism of the model.

⚠

Why does 2 operators produce fewer units than 1? (29 printers: 50,994 vs 51,030)

This is a characteristic behaviour of discrete-event simulation that pure theory cannot expose. With 1 operator, the system is entirely sequential — the single worker follows a fixed, unambiguous route through the printer farm with no resource contention. With 2 operators, both workers share the same buffers, carriers, and CP Lab stations. At certain moments, both operators arrive at the same resource simultaneously, creating micro-level queuing and a brief deadlock. Accumulated over 252 simulated days, this results in ~36 fewer units produced.

From 3 operators onward the system stabilises at 51,030 enough workers exist to absorb contention without competing for the same resource at the same instant. This non-linear behaviour is invisible to analytical calculation and is one of the strongest arguments for simulation-based capacity planning.

Key Insight

The value of simulation over theory

Theory said 28 printers + 4 operators. Simulation revealed that 28 printers can never reach 50,000 units regardless of operators, and that the fix is one additional printer not more headcount. The cost difference is significant: one printer vs. one full-time operator.

Key Findings &
Lessons Learned

  • 28 printers hit an invisible ceiling — no matter how many operators

    With 28 printers, output plateaued at 49,140 units/year from 3 operators onwards. Adding further operators produced zero improvement. The constraint was the printer count itself — a ceiling the theoretical formula never predicted.

    Theory calculated 28 printers as sufficient. Simulation revealed they fall 860 units short of the 50,000 annual target. The decision to add one printer (29 total) was only possible because simulation exposed this hidden limit before any capital was committed.

  • One extra printer unlocks the annual target — not more operators

    Adding a single 29th printer raised output to 51,030 units/year — exceeding the 50,000 target by 1,030 units. This was achieved with just 1 operator, contradicting the theoretical recommendation of 4 operators.

    The correct investment was printer hardware, not headcount. A single additional printer costs a one-time capital outlay; an extra operator represents a recurring annual salary. Simulation identified the more cost-effective solution.

  • The oven (100%) is the CP Lab bottleneck — doubling it doubles throughput exactly

    In the CP Lab baseline, the oven ran at 100% utilisation. Adding a second oven alongside a second Pick-by-Light station doubled output from 4,962 to 9,922 parts in the same simulation period. Pick-by-Light held steady at 77% in both scenarios.

    The result confirms Goldratt's principle directly: the oven was the binding constraint, and investing in any other station would have produced no gain. Doubling precisely the bottleneck resource produced a proportional doubling of output.

  • Non-linear behaviour: 2 operators briefly underperform 1 (29 printers)

    With 29 printers, 2 operators produced 50,994 units — 36 fewer than the 51,030 achieved with just 1 operator. This counter-intuitive result emerges from micro-level resource contention in discrete-event simulation: two workers occasionally compete for the same carrier or buffer simultaneously.

    No analytical formula can predict this behaviour. It is a direct demonstration of why simulation-based planning is superior to spreadsheet-based capacity models for systems with shared resources and stochastic timing.

  • Simulation prevents costly over- and under-investment simultaneously

    Theory overestimated operators needed (4 vs. 1 sufficient) and underestimated printers needed (28 vs. 29 required). Both errors would have been expensive: one wasted headcount, the other missed the annual production target entirely.

    According to Sargent (2013), validated simulation models provide decision confidence that analytical models cannot. In this case, simulation corrected two simultaneous errors in opposite directions — exactly the scenario where its value is highest.
