Injection Moulding vs.
3D Printing

Strategic Comparison

Injection moulding requires custom tooling costing $10,000–$250,000 with lead times of 4–12 weeks (BigRep, 2025). Once the mold is built, cycle times are fast (30–90 seconds), making it efficient for high-volume runs exceeding 10,000 units (Sovol, 2026).

However, design changes require expensive mould rework (Sovol, 2026). Additive manufacturing offers zero tooling investment, geometric complexity at no extra cost, and design-test cycles under 24 hours. In addition, according to BigRep (2025), the lead time for 3D printing is measured in hours or days, not weeks.

For production the present estimated production run, Additive Manufacturing offers lower total cost of ownership despite higher per-part cost, because the multi-thousand-dollar tooling investment is eliminated (Christensen et al., 2018)

Strategic implication: Additive manufacturing will not replace injection moulding for commodity, high-volume products. However, for small-batch, modular, customised products precisely the market segment occupied by "Phones-R-Us", additive manufacturing is not merely an alternative; it is the superior technology (Christensen, 2019).

Why Additive Manufacturing?

⚡

Design Iteration Speed

Complete design-test cycle in under 24 hours. 8 print configurations (C1–C8) tested equivalent to $160,000 in injection mould tooling (BigRep, 2025).

🔧

Complexity at Zero Cost

Traceability codes, snap-fit variants, and internal features require no additional tooling. Complexity is free in additive manufacturing.

📦

On-Demand Production

Just-in-time manufacturing reduces working capital and obsolescence risk (Pullagura et al., 2025). Print casings as needed, not months in advance.

Supply Chain Variability

The Multiple-Supplier
Problem

Components from different suppliers, all manufactured to the same engineering drawing — are not truly interchangeable.

According to Hosseinzadeh and Taghipour (2024), manufacturing systems increasingly rely on multi-sourcing as a resilience strategy. However, this introduces geometric variability between suppliers.

Song, Dong and Xu (2014) established that supplier base size directly affects total expected cost and quality consistency.

Our Solution:

Statistical interchangeability guarantees, achieved through:

  • Qualified process parameters (Configuration 7)
  • In-process metrology (thickness measurement at CP Lab Station 1)
  • Digital traceability (each casing carries its print parameters)

This approach transforms the multiple-supplier problem from a quality risk into a managed process capability (Montgomery, 2019).

Industry
4.0

Industry 4.0, the Fourth Industrial Revolution, redefines manufacturing through interconnected systems. According to Pullagura et al. (2025), the core design principles include:

The Four Pillars

01InteroperabilitySeamless communication between devices, machines, and systems: Cura/Bambu Slicer → G-code → Printer → Metrology data → CP Lab validation
02Information TransparencyData integrity and accessibility across the manufacturing ecosystem: Traceability codes, documented print parameters for each configuration and data gathering from the printers and the CP Lab
03Decentralized Decision-MakingReal-time responses using edge computing and local intelligence: Printer firmware adjustments and slicer parameter optimization per geometry
04Technical AssistanceSimulation results (Tecnomatix) guiding production planning decisions

Pullagura et al. (2025) — Industry 4.0 Design Principles

Industry 4.0 implementation has demonstrated measurable benefits: IBM reports that smart manufacturing approaches improve defect detection by approximately 50% and yield by approximately 20% compared to conventional production (Matrikon, 2025).

Enabling Technologies

Two enabling technologies underpin our manufacturing strategy: Industrial IoT and Digital Twins.

Industrial IoT
(IIoT)

IIoT deploys connected sensors on equipment to collect operational data for analytics and optimisation (Matrikon, 2025).

In our printing farm, IIoT enables:

  • Real-time temperature monitoring during prints
  • Filament usage tracking (18g per casing set in C7)
  • Machine health dashboards for the 185 printers required to meet 50,000 units/year

Digital Twin

A digital twin is a virtual model of a physical asset, process, or system, fed by real-time sensor data (Matrikon, 2025).

In our project, the digital twin appears in two forms:

  • Tecnomatix Plant Simulation: Virtual model of the CP Lab assembly line, calculating Takt Time (127 sec/unit) and determining that 185 printers are needed in parallel
  • Process Digital Twin: Our documented C7 parameters serve as a "baseline twin" — any future parameter change can be tested virtually before physical printing

Hierarchical Relationship

Industry 4.0 is the overarching strategic vision → Smart Manufacturing is the implementation pathway → Digital Twins are the enabling technology that makes both possible (Huawei Cloud Community, 2025).
