3D Design and Print

Complete design process in Fusion 360, from engineering drawing to print-ready files. This entry documents every design decision, dimensional iteration, and the critical link between CAD parameters and assembly line performance.

01 — Initial concept

From the Engineering drawing
to the 3D model

The project began with full engineering drawings for both top and bottom phone casings. No tolerances were specified initially, only the requirement that the final parts must successfully navigate the complete CP Lab assembly line, including dimensional verification stations, component insertion tests, and seamless fit with the pre-existing "master parts" provided with the production line.

Both casings were modeled from scratch in Fusion 360 using direct modeling. When drawing information was incomplete (e.g., certain internal radii), design decisions followed the pattern of similar geometric features elsewhere on the casing to maintain visual and functional consistency.

A key early insight: print parameters would ultimately determine whether the parts passed the thickness tolerance (1.6-2.2mm differential between two measurement points on the bottom case) and component fit requirements.

🏆 FINAL DECISION

Why Configuration 7 was selected

After eight distinct print configurations, over fifteen print attempts, and iterative CAD refinements across two different printer platforms (Ultimaker and Elegoo Neptune),Configuration 7 was selected as the optimal parameter set for manufacturing the phone casings. This decision was based on four critical criteria directly linked to the CP Lab assembly line requirements, supported by empirical measurements.

The four critical criteria directly linked to the CP Lab assembly line requirements are:

  • ✅ Dimensional accuracy: The thickness measurement of 1.7mm fell within the required 1.6-2.2mm tolerance range, verified by the CP Lab inspection station.
  • ✅ Fit with master parts: The casings assembled seamlessly with the reference master parts provided with the production line, no excessive force or loose fitting.
  • ✅ Support-free printing: By eliminating supports entirely (using variable layer height and overhang speed optimisation), post-processing time was reduced while surface quality was preserved.
  • ✅ CP Lab validation: The parts successfully passed all assembly line tests: dimensional inspection, component insertion, pick-by-light recognition, and press-fitting.

Why not configuration 8?
While Configuration 8 (60% infill) eliminated the witness line visible on C7, it worsened the fit with master parts demonstrating that higher infill does not always improve dimensional accuracy. The 15% infill in C7 produced a more predictable, balanced shrinkage profile that preserved geometric fidelity to the master parts.

📌 Key takeaway: The optimal print configuration is not necessarily the one with the highest infill or finest layer height. Rather, it is the balance of parameters that produces consistent, repeatable results that meet the specific requirements of the assembly line in this case, 15% infill, no supports, variable layer height, and optimised overhang speeds.
